# Songwriting Craft Reference

Use this file when drafting or revising lyrics.

## Core Decisions

- Promise: what emotional change should the listener feel by the final chorus?
- Speaker: who is singing, to whom, and from what distance in time?
- Hook: the title phrase or central image that can survive repetition.
- Contrast: verse details should move; chorus should crystallize.
- Constraint: one image family is stronger than five unrelated metaphors.

## Reliable Structures

- Pop single: `[Verse 1] [Pre-Chorus] [Chorus] [Verse 2] [Pre-Chorus] [Chorus] [Bridge] [Final Chorus]`.
- Ballad: `[Verse 1] [Verse 2] [Chorus] [Verse 3] [Bridge] [Final Chorus] [Outro]`.
- Rap or hip-hop: `[Intro] [Verse 1] [Hook] [Verse 2] [Hook] [Bridge or Breakdown] [Final Hook]`.
- EDM: `[Verse] [Pre-Chorus] [Build] [Drop] [Verse 2] [Build] [Drop] [Outro]`.
- Folk or story song: `[Verse 1] [Verse 2] [Chorus] [Verse 3] [Chorus] [Outro]`.
- Bilingual pop: keep one primary language; use the second language for hook phrases, call-and-response, or a bridge.

## Line Craft

- Keep most sung lines between 5 and 12 syllables in English unless the genre needs longer flow.
- Put plain language near the hook. Save ornate images for verses and bridge.
- Use slant rhyme, internal rhyme, and repeated vowel shapes instead of forcing perfect rhyme.
- Make verse 2 add new information. Do not restate verse 1 with synonyms.
- Let the bridge change perspective: confession, reversal, vow, memory, or decision.

## Chinese Lyric Notes

- For detailed Mandarin lyric revision, read `chinese-lyric-craft.md`.
- Write for natural Mandarin phrasing, not translated English syntax.
- Favor vivid concrete nouns and verbs over abstract slogan language.
- Keep line lengths compact so Suno has room to phrase them musically.
- Rhyme can be loose. Tone, cadence, and repeated key words often matter more than perfect end rhyme.
- Avoid overusing generic phrases such as "stars", "dreams", "lonely night", and "heartbreak" unless they are made specific.

## Anti-Cliche Checks

Replace lines that depend on:

- Generic weather as emotion with no fresh detail.
- "Lost in the night", "broken wings", "fire inside", "chasing dreams", or similar stock phrases.
- Abstract declarations without a scene.
- Chorus lines that could fit any song in the genre.
- Forced inspirational slogans.

## Revision Moves

- If the chorus is weak, rewrite it around the title and one concrete verb.
- If the verse is flat, add a time, place, object, or action.
- If the lyric feels too dense, remove adjectives before removing nouns.
- If the rhyme sounds childish, loosen the rhyme and strengthen rhythm.
- If the Suno result rushes, shorten sections and reduce syllables.

## Refinement Passes

Run one pass by default, up to three when the user wants polish:

- Tighten: cut filler, repeated meaning, and ornamental adjectives.
- Strengthen: replace generic emotion with image, action, or a sharper hook phrase.
- Flow and ear: read aloud, smooth stress, shorten rushed lines, and keep parallel lines close in length.

Preserve the song's voice during refinement. Do not introduce a new metaphor system, character, or plot turn unless the user asks for a rewrite.

## Genre-Switch Songs

For a song that starts in one style and changes into another:

- Make the switch emotionally earned, not just a gimmick.
- Foreshadow the second style with rhythm, lyric density, or imagery before the switch.
- Use a clear transition tag such as `[Breakdown]`, `[Build]`, or `[Beat Switch]`.
- Tighten rhyme and syllable control before a fast section; messy language becomes more obvious at speed.
